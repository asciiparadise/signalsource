# Geogaddi

## Artist

[[Boards of Canada Hub|Boards of Canada]]

## Medium

Music album

## Why It Feels Important

I have never really thought of *Geogaddi* as an occult record in the simple sense.

That is the surface, of course. The numbers, the hidden messages, the cult references, the Satanic panic atmosphere, the sense that something is coded into the music. But underneath all of that, it feels more like a record about the moment wonder becomes unsafe.

If *Music Has the Right to Children* is childhood remembered through sunlight and tape hiss, *Geogaddi* is what happens when the same childhood begins to cast a shadow.

The world is still beautiful.

But now it feels watched.

## The Shadow Arrives

What makes the album unsettling is not that it is openly dark all the time. It is more slippery than that.

Some of the melodies are still lovely. Some tracks still have that soft, almost educational glow. But the context has changed. The same warmth now feels suspicious. The same repetition becomes ritual. The same childlike quality becomes vulnerable.

That is probably why the album works so well.

It does not abandon the earlier Boards of Canada world. It infects it.

## Occult Surface, Emotional Core

The occult material matters, but I do not think it is the final meaning.

The references to cults, numbers, hidden structures and diabolic imagery create a pressure around the music. They make the listener feel that something is being withheld, or that a system is operating just outside view.

Maybe there is no single system to decode.

Maybe the feeling of decoding is the point.

## Signal Source Reading

*Geogaddi* is not really about evil.

It is about the realization that wonder has a shadow.

That is a much more frightening idea, because it means the darkness was not imported from somewhere else. It was already inside the same system as the beauty.

This is why the album sits so naturally beside Twin Peaks. Both works understand that innocence and horror can occupy the same room.

## Influence on ASCII Paradise

A reminder that darkness does not need to be loud or obvious.

Sometimes it is more powerful when it enters through symbols, repetition, small distortions and the feeling that something familiar has shifted slightly out of alignment.
