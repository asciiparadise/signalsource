# Lost Media

Lost media matters here because absence can become part of the artwork.

I do not mean that every missing tape or unavailable film is automatically important. Most things disappear simply because the world is messy. But sometimes the missing object starts to gather meaning around itself. People search for it, describe it, misremember it, reconstruct it, pass around fragments, and eventually the absence becomes active.

Boards of Canada are very close to this feeling.

The early tapes, the scarce releases, the half-known history around Hexagon Sun. It all creates the sense that the official discography is only the visible part of something larger.

## Why It Matters

Lost media creates a strange kind of attention.

You are not only listening or watching. You are imagining the missing context. Who had it? What did it sound like? Was it important, or did scarcity make it feel important?

Maybe that question can never be fully answered.

But the uncertainty is part of the atmosphere.

## Signal Source Reading

Lost media is not just about recovering missing objects.

It is about the emotional charge created by incompleteness. A gap can become a symbol. A missing tape can become a mythology. A fragment can sometimes feel more powerful than the complete thing would have been.
