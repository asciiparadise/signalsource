# Colour Theory

## Why It Belongs Here

Colour theory belongs in Signal Source because colour is one of the fastest ways a work tells the body what kind of world it has entered.

Before meaning, before plot, before interpretation, colour changes the room.

A cold blue can make a scene feel distant or technological. A deep red can turn space into ritual, danger or flesh. Orange can feel radioactive, nostalgic, autumnal, desert-like or apocalyptic depending on context. Green can become nature, illness, night vision, finance, decay or occult glow.

Colour is never only surface.

It is atmosphere made visible.

## What We Actually Know

Colour theory is the study and practice of how colours relate to each other and how they affect perception.

Important areas include:

- hue
- saturation
- value / brightness
- contrast
- complementary colours
- analogous colours
- warm and cool colour relationships
- colour harmony
- colour symbolism
- cultural colour associations

In design and art, colour theory is both technical and emotional. It helps structure hierarchy, mood, readability, tension and identity.

## Colour as Emotional Architecture

A palette is a kind of architecture.

It builds a world before the viewer has time to analyse it. This is especially clear in cinema and album artwork. *Blade Runner 2049* would not feel the same without its grey fog, sterile white, deep blue and radioactive orange. Boards of Canada would not feel the same without washed-out yellows, faded greens, sun-bleached browns and degraded broadcast tones.

Colour can make time visible.

Fresh colour feels present.

Faded colour feels remembered.

Over-saturated colour can feel artificial, ecstatic or sick.

## Signal Source Reading

Colour matters because it bypasses explanation.

You can explain why red means danger or ritual, but the body often knows before the explanation arrives.

That is why colour belongs next to [[Symbolic Thinking]]. It is not a symbol in the same way as a hexagon or a serpent, but it still carries meaning across works.

Colour is mood, signal and memory at once.
