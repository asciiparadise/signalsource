# Hi Scores

## Artist

[[Boards of Canada Hub|Boards of Canada]]

## Release Type

EP

## Why It Matters

*Hi Scores* feels like the first really clear public signal.

With *Twoism*, the world is still partly private, still close to the early-tape fog. But *Hi Scores* feels sharper. More deliberate. You can hear the Boards of Canada identity moving into focus: the beats, the softened melodies, the strange brightness, the sense that something playful is also slightly uncanny.

It is not as expansive as *Music Has the Right to Children*, but it already understands the language.

## The Public Signal

There is something almost instructional about this release.

Not instructional in the sense of teaching, but in the sense that it seems to establish rules. This is the rhythm. This is the warmth. This is the haze. This is the way childhood, machines and memory can sit together without explaining themselves.

Maybe that is why it feels so important. It does not simply come before the later records. It announces the method.

## Emotional Texture

The music has that early Boards of Canada balance between play and unease.

It can feel bright on the surface, but the brightness is never clean. There is always a little dust on it. Something in the sound feels aged before its time, as though the music has arrived already archived.

That quality becomes central to almost everything later.

## Signal Source Reading

*Hi Scores* is the moment where the signal becomes legible.

The mythology is not fully there yet, but the emotional technology is. It shows how a beat, a melody, a voice, a fragment of age and a strange sense of distance can create a whole world from very little.
