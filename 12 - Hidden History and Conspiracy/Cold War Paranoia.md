# Cold War Paranoia

## Evidence Status

**Documented historical atmosphere / cultural condition.**

Cold War paranoia is not a single event or programme.

It is the psychological and cultural climate produced by nuclear threat, espionage, propaganda, ideological conflict, surveillance, secrecy and the possibility of total destruction.

Some fears were grounded in real events.

Some became exaggerated, displaced or mythologised.

## Why It Belongs Here

Cold War paranoia belongs in Signal Source because it is one of the major emotional engines behind the vault.

So much of the atmosphere here comes from the Cold War afterimage:

- number stations
- public information films
- bunkers
- radar towers
- emergency broadcasts
- secret agencies
- nuclear tests
- cults and end-times thinking
- educational optimism turning into dread

Boards of Canada, *Twin Peaks: The Return*, Montauk mythology, number stations and *Tomorrow's Harvest* all carry some trace of this world.

## What We Actually Know

The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension after the Second World War, mainly between the United States and its allies and the Soviet Union and its allies.

Its cultural atmosphere included:

- nuclear fear
- espionage and counterespionage
- propaganda
- surveillance
- proxy wars
- civil-defence culture
- space-race technological ambition
- ideological polarisation
- fear of infiltration

This was not simply imaginary. Nuclear weapons, covert operations and state secrecy were real. But the emotional atmosphere often exceeded what ordinary people could verify.

## What Became Mythology

Cold War paranoia became a symbolic language.

The spy. The bunker. The coded transmission. The redacted document. The strange signal. The radar installation. The school film explaining catastrophe in a calm voice.

These images remain powerful because they combine rational modern systems with primitive fear.

The world is ending, but there is a diagram for it.

## Signal Source Reading

Cold War paranoia matters because it makes modernity feel haunted.

Technology no longer means only progress. It also means surveillance, destruction, secrecy and control.

That is why the sound of an old broadcast or the image of a radar tower can feel so loaded. It carries a whole emotional architecture: optimism, fear, state power and apocalypse sitting together in the same signal.
