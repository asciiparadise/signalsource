# Corduroy Psychedelia - Boards of Canada, Hauntology, and the PBS Unconscious

## Type
Article / Essay Note

## Source

SEREPTIE, "Corduroy Psychedelia: On Boards of Canada, Hauntology, and the PBS Unconscious", published March 27, 2026.

## Core Idea

The essay proposes "corduroy psychedelia" as a way to describe a shared public-broadcasting childhood atmosphere: sick days, PBS, educational films, analog television, geometric animation, civic realism, institutional warmth, boredom, fever, and the strange psychic aperture through which Boards of Canada later becomes legible.

The article frames Boards of Canada not simply as nostalgic music, but as music that reveals how certain media memories were already haunted: by deindustrialization, lost futures, therapeutic utopianism, apocalypse, childhood, ecology, and occult undertones.

## Key Concepts

### Corduroy Psychedelia

A soft, domestic, educational, intimate and subtly psychedelic atmosphere associated with 1970s and 1980s public broadcasting. It is not glamour, spectacle or overt counterculture, but something quieter: corduroy, reading rugs, sick days, public television, school films, PBS, Sesame Street, geometry, nature documentaries and feverish daytime viewing.

### PBS Unconscious

The essay identifies a shared anglophone public-broadcasting unconscious linking PBS, BBC, Channel 4, Canadian public broadcasting, the National Film Board of Canada and the BBC Radiophonic Workshop. Boards of Canada are positioned as definitive artists of this media unconscious.

### Screen Memories

The essay uses the idea of screen memories to explain the emotional force of incomplete, degraded, half-remembered media. The value lies partly in the gaps. Boards of Canada supply memories that feel personal even when they were never personally lived.

### Cult of Childhood

The article expands on the idea, attributed to Simon Reynolds, that *Music Has the Right to Children* is devoted to a "cult of childhood". Childhood appears as both arcadian and ominous: a threshold of wonder, but also regression, unbounded desire and danger.

### Arcadian / Apocalyptic Pairing

One of the essay's strongest ideas is that every cult of childlike wonder contains its shadow: impending catastrophe, Satanic apocalypse, ecological collapse, cult violence or historical ruin. This helps explain the movement from *Music Has the Right to Children* through *Geogaddi* and into *Tomorrow's Harvest*.

### The Past Inside the Present

The essay connects this directly to "Music Is Math" and the phrase "the past inside the present". Memory is not retrieval of a dead object, but recurrence, loop, residue and haunting.

## Boards of Canada Reading

The essay presents Boards of Canada as artists of:

- childhood memory
- public broadcasting
- analogue warmth
- degraded educational media
- nature and ecology
- occult undertones
- lost futures
- therapeutic utopianism
- apocalyptic reversal
- communal but diffuse nostalgia

It argues that their music feels both deeply personal and strangely impersonal: a memory that is not yours, yet somehow belongs to you.

## Album Arc

### Music Has the Right to Children

Childhood as vivid and lost. Arcadian, warm, faceless, communal, intimate. The album is treated as the centre of the cult of childhood.

### Geogaddi

The apocalyptic and occult turn. Sesame Street and educational sources remain, but the atmosphere becomes darker: sacred conspiracy, subliminal diabolism, found media, environmental dread and cosmic cruelty.

### The Campfire Headphase

A partial reprieve: warmth, road movement, folk texture and open landscapes.

### Tomorrow's Harvest

The world after the catastrophe. The essay reads this as the terminal end of the BoC arc: brutalist ruins, ecological collapse, empty playgrounds and prophetic aftermath.

## Vault Interpretation

This article belongs in the Boards of Canada section, but it also belongs to the wider Signal Source project because it gives language to a recurring aesthetic: childhood media as a haunted system.

It is especially useful for ASCII Paradise because it articulates how analogue technology, memory, education, geometry, nature, apocalypse and occult atmosphere can coexist without becoming literal fantasy. It is not simply nostalgia. It is nostalgia with a shadow, and maybe that is why it feels alive.
