# Mark Fisher

## Why He Belongs Here

Mark Fisher belongs in Signal Source because he gives language to the feeling that the future disappeared.

That sounds dramatic, but it is probably the simplest way to state his importance here. Fisher's writing on hauntology, capitalist realism, cultural exhaustion and lost futures sits very close to Boards of Canada, public broadcasting memory, degraded media and the strange sadness of futures that once seemed possible but never arrived.

He is not only useful because he wrote about music.

He is useful because he understood cultural atmosphere.

## What We Actually Know

Mark Fisher was a British cultural theorist, critic and writer.

He is especially associated with:

- *Capitalist Realism*
- hauntology
- lost futures
- post-punk and electronic music criticism
- the blog *k-punk*
- the political and psychological effects of late capitalism

His work often asks why contemporary culture feels stuck repeating, recycling or mourning older futures.

## Hauntology

Fisher's hauntology is central to this vault.

It is not simply nostalgia.

It is the presence of lost futures in the present. The sense that something promised by the past did not happen, but still haunts culture as residue: old broadcasts, public-service optimism, analogue media, abandoned modernism, children's television, library music, worn-out utopianism.

That is almost a direct bridge to [[Boards of Canada Hub]].

## Signal Source Reading

Fisher helps explain why some old media feels so emotionally loaded.

A faded educational film is not only old.

It carries a dead idea of the future.

A warped synth is not only nostalgic.

It may sound like a future that failed to become real.

That is why Fisher matters here. He gives the vault a way to think about memory politically and culturally, not only personally.
