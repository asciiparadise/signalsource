# Jack Parsons and the Babalon Working

## Core Idea

Jack Parsons is one of those figures who feels almost too Signal Source to be real.

Rocket scientist by day, Thelemite occultist by night. One of the founders of modern rocketry, but also a man trying to open spiritual doors in the Mojave Desert with L. Ron Hubbard. It sounds invented, but the unsettling part is that the basic history is real.

That is exactly why Mark Frost uses him so well.

## The Real-World Charge

Parsons connects science and occultism in a way that feels almost mythological.

He helped push humanity toward space, but he was also trying to contact or manifest forces beyond ordinary reality. Rockets and rituals. Jet propulsion and Babalon. The future and the abyss sitting in the same room.

I have to admit, that combination feels very close to the deeper logic of *Twin Peaks*.

Modernity does not replace myth.

It gives myth new machines.

## Frost's Twin Peaks Use

In *The Secret History of Twin Peaks*, Frost takes Parsons' Babalon Working and places it near the dimensional wound opened by the atomic bomb.

That is the brilliant move.

The Trinity Test tears reality open.

Parsons' ritual holds the door.

Maybe that is too neat historically, but as mythology it is almost perfect. Science creates the rupture. Occult ambition reaches through it.

## Judy / Babalon

Parsons wanted to summon Babalon, a destructive and divine feminine force from Crowley's Thelemic system.

Inside the *Twin Peaks* mythology, this begins to feel uncomfortably close to Judy: the extreme negative force, the mothering void, the thing behind BOB.

Frost is not just adding an occult reference. He is suggesting that American history itself may have helped invite the horror in.

## Owl Cave Ring

Frost also connects Parsons to the [[Owl Cave Ring]], tying the real-world occult history directly to the physical mythology of the series.

That matters because it stops being abstract.

The ring becomes the object that joins desert ritual, Lodge forces, Thelema and Twin Peaks itself.

## Signal Source Reading

Jack Parsons matters because he collapses categories that are usually kept separate.

Science, magic, military technology, sex ritual, cosmic ambition, American desert mythology.

In Frost's hands, he becomes a perfect symbol of human hubris: the belief that we can open doors without understanding what might come through.
