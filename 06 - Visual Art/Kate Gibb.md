# Kate Gibb

## Medium

Printmaking / silkscreen / illustration / music design

## Why She Belongs Here

Kate Gibb belongs in Signal Source because her work makes process visible.

That matters more than it might sound. A lot of contemporary design tries to hide the making. It wants the surface to be clean, repeatable, controlled, almost frictionless. Gibb's work goes the other way. You can feel ink, pressure, layering, misregistration, accident, colour shifts, texture and hand decision.

It feels made.

That is probably why it resonates. There is a human warmth in it, but not in a sentimental way. More like the image has been allowed to breathe and misbehave a little.

## Silkscreen as Method

Gibb is often associated with silkscreen printing, and that is not just a technical detail.

The process is the work.

Silkscreen has a physical logic: layers build up, colours interact, opacity changes, edges slip, pressure varies, and small mistakes become part of the final image. In a digital file, error usually feels like failure. In print, error can become character.

That difference is important for this vault.

Signal Source keeps returning to degraded tape, VHS texture, old photographs, photocopies, static, worn print and damaged archives. Gibb connects to that same instinct, but through colour and surface rather than melancholy alone.

## Happy Accidents

Her work often leans into what could be called bad printing: hiccups, misalignment, ink behaviour, imperfect layers.

But the point is not mess for its own sake.

The point is that chance can make the image feel alive. A print that is too perfect can become dead very quickly. A print with small accidents carries the trace of a moment. It happened once, in that way, with that pressure, that ink, that decision.

This is where her work connects to [[Decay]] and [[Signal and Noise]], even though the mood is often brighter.

Imperfection becomes memory.

## The Chemical Brothers

Her work for The Chemical Brothers is probably where many people first met her visual language.

The sleeves and associated imagery create a world of vivid colour, movement, psychedelia and layered forms. They feel connected to electronic music without trying to illustrate the sound too literally.

That is difficult to do well.

A lot of music design becomes decoration. Gibb's work feels more like a visual equivalent of rhythm and pressure. Colours collide. Shapes pulse. Layers behave almost like tracks in a mix.

## Friendly but Strange

One thing I like about Gibb's work is that it can feel friendly without becoming simple.

There is brightness, tactility and warmth, but also abstraction and complexity. The images do not shut the viewer out. They invite you in through colour and texture, then keep shifting once you are there.

That combination is useful for Signal Source because not everything in the vault needs to be dark.

The important thing is not darkness.

The important thing is depth.

## Process Against Digital Smoothness

Gibb feels especially valuable now because so much visual culture has become too smooth.

Everything can be corrected, aligned, sharpened, rendered, polished. The result can be impressive but strangely lifeless. Her work reminds me that the hand, the studio, the physical surface and the accident still matter.

In that sense, her work sits beside the vault's love of analogue media.

Not because analogue is automatically better.

Because process leaves traces.

## Signal Source Reading

Kate Gibb matters because she shows that visual systems do not need to be clean to be coherent.

They can be layered, imperfect, playful, tactile and still have a strong internal logic.

That is useful for ASCII Paradise too. A world can be built through recurring symbols and textures, but it should not feel sealed in plastic. It should carry evidence of process. It should have marks, mistakes, pressure, ink, accidents, and the feeling that someone actually made it.

## Influence on ASCII Paradise

The lesson is to let the process show.

Do not smooth everything away.

Let colour layers collide. Let texture carry memory. Let accidents stay if they make the work feel more alive.
